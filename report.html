Introduction

Every year, millions of newborns die within their first 28 days of life — a tragedy that is not distributed equally across the world. Neonatal mortality — the death of a child in the first month of life — remains one of the starkest indicators of global inequality. While wealthier nations have driven rates down to near zero, countries in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia continue to record rates that exceed 40 deaths per 1,000 live births.

This report examines UNICEF data to explore:

  • How neonatal mortality is distributed across the globe in 2023
  • How the global average has changed since 1990
  • Whether access to safe drinking water is linked to survival outcomes
  • Whether economic development shapes a country’s ability to protect its newborns
  • Which countries face the highest burden today

Data source: UNICEF Global Indicators — 199 countries across multiple decades.

# Transform 1: Map data
map_data = (
    indicator_1
    .filter(pl.col("time_period") == 2023)
    .select(["alpha_3_code", "country", "obs_value"])
    .rename({"obs_value": "neonatal_rate"})
    .sort("neonatal_rate", descending=True)
)

# Transform 2: Time series
timeseries_data = (
    indicator_1
    .filter(pl.col("time_period") >= 1990)
    .group_by("time_period")
    .agg(pl.col("obs_value").mean().alias("avg_neonatal_rate"))
    .sort("time_period")
)

# Transform 3: Water scatter
neonatal_2023 = (
    indicator_1
    .filter(pl.col("time_period") == 2023)
    .select(["alpha_3_code", "obs_value"])
    .rename({"obs_value": "neonatal_rate"})
)

water_2023 = (
    indicator_2
    .filter(pl.col("time_period") == 2023)
    .select(["alpha_3_code", "obs_value"])
    .rename({"obs_value": "water_access"})
)

water_scatter = neonatal_2023.join(water_2023, on="alpha_3_code", how="inner")

# Transform 4: Top 10 bar chart
top10 = (
    indicator_1

Visualisations

Global Distribution of Neonatal Mortality (2023)

The map below shows how dramatically neonatal mortality varies by geography. Sub-Saharan Africa stands out with the deepest red shading, while Europe, North America, and Australia appear in light green tones reflecting rates well below 5 per 1,000 live births. The stark visual contrast underscores just how unequal newborn survival outcomes remain in 2023.

Global Trend in Neonatal Mortality (1990–2023)

Despite the scale of the problem, there is genuine cause for optimism. The global average neonatal mortality rate fell from approximately 24 deaths per 1,000 live births in 1990 to around 12 in 2023 — a reduction of more than half over three decades. This reflects sustained investment in maternal health, vaccination programmes, and improved birth care globally.

Water Access and Neonatal Mortality (2023)

Access to safe piped drinking water is a fundamental building block of child health. The scatter plot below reveals a clear inverse relationship — as the proportion of a population with piped water access increases, neonatal mortality falls. Countries with near-universal water access above 90% cluster below 5 deaths per 1,000, while those below 20% access frequently exceed 25.

Countries Facing the Highest Neonatal Mortality (2023)

A small number of countries account for a disproportionate share of global neonatal deaths. South Sudan records the highest rate at over 40 deaths per 1,000 live births, more than eight times the global average. Pakistan, Somalia, Afghanistan, and Niger all exceed 33 deaths per 1,000, highlighting the concentration of this crisis in fragile and conflict-affected states.

Conclusion

The data tells a clear story. Global neonatal mortality has halved since 1990, a remarkable achievement driven by investment in healthcare, sanitation, and economic growth. However, the progress has been deeply uneven.

Key findings from this analysis:

  • Geography matters enormously — Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia bear the greatest burden, while Europe and North America have largely eliminated the problem
  • Water access is a strong predictor — countries with piped water coverage above 90% almost always record mortality below 5 per 1,000
  • A small group of countries needs urgent attention — South Sudan, Pakistan, Somalia, Afghanistan, and Niger all exceed 33 deaths per 1,000

Reducing preventable neonatal deaths is not simply a healthcare challenge — it is a question of infrastructure, economics, and political will. The tools exist. The data shows where they are needed most.
